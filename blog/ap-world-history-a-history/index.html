AP World History: a History

May 2023

History is constantly changing, so it makes sense that College Board has been constantly updating their Advanced Placement World History course curriculum. The exam and time periods have changed many times since the course's introduction, but why has College Board made so many changes? Has this made it easier or more difficult for students to keep up? I'll explore the most notable changes that have been made to the AP World History curriculum over the 20+ years the course has been around.

Original 2002 Curriculum

In the 2001-2002 school year, AP World History was introduced, and its first exam was taken by over 20,000 students. (For comparison, the most recent 2022 exam was taken by over 300,000 students.) The mean score was 2.94.

2004 Changes

Exam weights and weeks of instruction time were updated. The date range of the "Foundations" period was specified.

Time Periods

Period Exam weight Instruction time
Foundations: 8000 BC - 600 AD 19-20% 7 weeks
600 AD - 1450 22% 8 weeks
1450 - 1750 19-20% 7 weeks
1750 - 1914 19-20% 7 weeks
1914 - present 19-20% 7 weeks

2011 Changes

Some period date ranges and exam weights were updated. Each period was also given a unique title. The DBQ now has 10 documents and the rubric further specifies the requirements for it.

Time Periods

Title Date range Exam weight
Technological and Environmntal Transformations - 600 BC 5%

Regional and Transregional Interactions 600 AD - 1450 20%
Global Intractions 1450 - 1750 20%
Industrialization and Global Integration 1750 - 1900 20%
Accelerating Global Change and Realignments 1900 - present 20%

Exam

Document-Based Question (DBQ)

  • 10 documents
  • Rubric:
    Description Possible points
    Acceptable thesis 1
    Addresses all documents and demonstrates understanding of at least all but one of the documents 1
    Supports thesis with evidence from at least all but one of the documents 2
    (Only supports thesis with evidence from at least all but two of the documents) (1)
    Analyzes bias or point of view in at least 2 documents 1
    Groups documents in 2 or 3 different ways depending on the prompt 1
    Identifies one type of appropriate additional document 1
    Expanded Core: expands beyond the basic core (can only earn points in the expanded core after earning all 7 previous points). This can be earned in a number of different ways, including:
    • A clear, analytical, and comprehensive thesis
    • Analyzes the bias or point of view of multiple documents
    • Analyzes document in additional ways: groupings, comparisons, synthesis
    • Uses evidence not found in the documents

2017 Changes

The new SAQ and LEQ questions replaced the comparative and change-over-time essays. The number of documents in DBQs was reduced to seven, and more time was given to complete it. The number of MCQs were reduced to 55, and were no longer about the memorization of facts, but about responding to stimulus, which can be a primary or secondary source that could include a passage, image, or data.

Exam

Multiple Choice Questions (MCQ)

  • 55 stimulus questions (questions that include responding to a primary or secondary source)
  • 55 minutes
  • 60 seconds per question
  • 40% of exam score

Short Answer Questions (SAQ)

  • 40 minutes
  • 3 questions
    • Question 1:
      • Periods 3-6
      • Given stimulus from a secondary source
    • Question 2:
      • Periods 3-6
      • Given stimulus from a primary source or image
    • Choose between:
      • Question 3:
        • Periods 1-3
        • No stimulus
      • Question 4:
        • Periods 4-6
        • No stimulus
  • 13 minutes and 20 seconds per question
  • 20% of exam score

Document-Based Question (DBQ)

  • 7 documents
  • 60 minutes
  • 15 minute built-in reading period
  • 25% of exam score
  • Rubric:
    Description Possible points
    Historically defensible thesis that responds to the prompt and establishes a line of reasoning 1
    Describes broader historical context relevant to the prompt 1
    Supports an argument in response to prompt using at least 6 documents 2
    (Only addresses topic of prompt using at least 3 documents) (1)
    Uses at least one additional piece of evidence not found in the documents and that is relevant to the prompt 1
    Explains how the document's point of view, purpose, historical situation, or audience is relevant to an argument for at least 3 documents 1
    Complexity: Demonstrates a complex understanding of the historical development of the prompt using evidence to corroborate, qualify, or modify an argument 1

Long Essay Question (LEQ)

  • 40 minutes
  • Choose from one of three questions that correspond to periods 1-2, 3-4, and 5-6
  • 15% of exam score
  • Rubric:
    Description Possible points
    Historically defensible thesis that responds to the prompt and establishes a line of reasoning 1
    Describes broader historical context relevant to the prompt 1
    Supports an argument in response to prompt using specific and relevant pieces of evidence 2
    (Only provides pieces of evidence relevant to the topic of the prompt) (1)
    Complexity: Demonstrates a complex understanding of the historical development of the prompt using evidence to corroborate, qualify, or modify an argument 2
    (Only uses historical reasoning to structure an argument that addresses the prompt) (1)

2020 Changes

The most recent changes to the curriculum were switching from time periods to units, and eliminating all content prior to 1200 AD.
