Continuous Rounding

April 1954

intro

How it Works

Instead of rounding up if a number is 5 or greater and rounding down if a number is 4 or smaller, continuous rounding starts at the right most place of a number and, moving right to left, continually rounds up each digit if the digit to the right of it is greater than four, stopping at the desired place to be rounded.

This method pretty much entirely relies upon the fact that 4 is the highest number that rounds a number down. If the digits after a 4 are continually rounded up, it will create a domino effect until it reaches the stopping place or a digit is rounded down. The method slightly increases the final number compared to normal rounding.

3.849 rounded to the nearest tenths place would normally be 3.8, but continually rounding it gives a result of 3.9.

Examples

Here's 7.6144582 continually rounded to the hundredths place step-by-step.

7.6144582

Starting at the right most number, 2, rounds the next digit down to 8.

7.614458

Here's where things really get different. The newest right-most digit, 8, rounds the digit to its left up to 6.

7.61446

Repeating the same process, the newest right-most digit of 6 rounds the digit to its left up to 5.

7.6145

5 rounds 4 up to 5...

7.615

...and 5 rounds 1 up to 2...

7.62

...giving a final result of 7.62!

Using normal rounding, it would be 7.61, but continually rounding it allows for a more comprehensive result to see the number as it really is overall.

Continuous rounding doesn't always produce a different result from normal rounding. Again, it depends on if a 4 is present in the number directly after the desired stopping place.

Here's 59,237 continually rounded to the nearest thousands place step-by-step.

59,237

Starting at the right most number of 7, it rounds 3 up to 4.

59,240

Then...the next number can't be rounded up anymore, so normal rules are used again, rounding 4 down to 0...

59,200

...and then 2 down to 0...

59,000

...giving a final result of 59,000.
